For the purposes of Evaluation 3, I will be conducting an examination of the University of California, Santa Barbara’s Davidson Library website. In order to do this, I will be utilizing Garrett’s 5 Planes of User Experience Design and applying those principles to the Davidson Library website.


Section I: Surface

To begin analyzing the Davidson Library website, we will start with the top-most layer of Garrett’s planes - the Surface. The surface deals with sensory design which is used to engage the user’s senses when interacting with the website. However, as it is difficult, or even impossible to engage all of the sense, there should naturally be a focus on how the eye engages with the site.
The layout of the site facilitates a smooth eye-flow from top to bottom and left to right, as the eye naturally wants to do (at least for a western reader).
There are open spaces that give the design an unrestricted feel and there are 4 distinct sections:

  1. Library Search
  2. Library Resources
  3. News
  4. Events
Each section has its own design and function.
The four sections clearly indicate that they contain different types of information. Each has links to to different resources available in their sections and is easily navigable.


Section II: Skeleton

The Skeleton layer deals with interface, navigation, and information design.
The interface, as mentioned previously, is divided into four sections. They each communicate different information, and each in different ways.

  • The first section has a unique box to the left of the search bar that allows the user to search different resources. The search bar changes correspondingly to the box selection.

  • The second section has two distinct bars of information - one on library hours, another on services available in the library. The second bar in the section has interactable links for the different services.

  • The third section, focusing on news, has a series of bees that are links to news articles with arrow buttons to the left and right of the section allowing the user to scroll through the listed announcements.

  • The fourth section, events and exhibitions, is divided into two blocks. The left block has a series of dates and the events listed for them. Each of the dates is a clickable link that takes the suer to a different page with more information. The right box has scrolling series of available exhibitions and is navigable via arrows on the left and right of the box. The navigation is mixed into the interface, effectively blending the two and gives it a natural feel.

Regarding information design, the sections clearly indicate the types of information that is available within them and is clearly indicated to the user.


Section III Structure

The third plane, Structure is split into Interaction Design, and Information Architecture and mostly approaches how the page is interacted with by users and how the system will respond to the user’s behavior.


One thing that stands out to me is that my instinct is to think that the library hours bar would have clickable links, but it has none and that feels odd. Instead, it has a drop-down menu that offers more information.


Another thing about the menu is that generally, when a user clicks off a menu, more often than note, that menu will close. The drop-down menu for the hours does not close until the “Close X” button is clicked, which also feels like a lack of thought.


Most everything else about the site, however, feels natural and operates as one would expect of it. The site is built so that it effectively conveys ideas and concepts that would be familiar to most internet users. As far as I have been able to tell, the site is able to predict user interaction and deal with responses. I am unable to make the site “break”.


Sections IV: Scope

According to Garrett, the question to be answered on the scope plane is “What are we going to make?” In order to determine to scope of the website, it must be divided into the product as functionality, and product as information.


The Functional Specifications of the website:
  • It must be able to be searchable
  • It must have lists of resources available
  • It must have a list of services that are available

Information Specifications of the website:
  • It must be UCSB branded
  • It must communicate the concept of the library

Regarding function, the website works as it would be expected to. The search function works as expected and allows the user to easily search different databases. Most of the resources and services available are listed on the main page or can be accessed through one or two links from therein page. The page clearly demonstrates the University’s brand, which is immediately communicated upon viewing the website, which is done through several outlets: the brand guide colors and the logo of the University.


The word "library" or some variation thereof is found 12 times on the main page. Upon viewing the page, the user is greeted with a host of services and a large search bar advertising “Search articles, books, and more”.


Sections V: Strategy

Garrett says that in order to examine the Strategy plane, we must pose the question: Why are we making this product?” And in order to answer that, we must ask two more questions: What does the library need to get out of the website and what do the users need to get out of it?

The library needs a website that facilitates a successful user experience and the users need a website that is a usable product, carefully designed with the intention of them utilizing it as a functional library website.

Ultimately, the website is designed with the user in mind, allowing it to facilitate a successful user experience. From the user's point of view, it is easy to navigate and make sue of the resources and services offered by the library. The site is responsive and interactive and the different sections are clearly marked. It contains an effective underlying strategy which sets the rest of the website up for success.
